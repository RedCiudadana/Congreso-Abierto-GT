We Make Open-Source Tools for Open-Gov

The Participatory Politics Foundation is a 501(c)(3) non-profit organization with a mission to increase civic engagement.

We believe that the Web has created an unprecedented opportunity for individuals to educate themselves in public affairs and amplify political actions. Every day, our lives abound in political feelings and opinions. Not just on Election Day.

Since 2007, PPF has made some of the leading sites on the Web for government transparency and accountability. Our work is free of charge, open-source, non-partisan, and non-profit. Our speciality is taking government data and making it more user-friendly:

 
  • The most-visited not-for-profit website in the world for tracking, understanding, and contacting the U.S. Congress
  • Uniquely combines official government info with news & blog coverage, campaign contribution data, and engagement tools
  • A key piece of civic infrastructure, with up to 1 million visits per month, 2.75 million page views per month, and 200,000 users
 
  • Takes the proven OpenCongress model of transparency down to government at any level: state, county, city, and local
  • A much-needed public resource, combining campaign contribution data, public comments, and free participation tools
  • Currently in beta, we seek to roll out to all 50 U.S. states and beyond by the November 2012 federal elections
 
  • Our sibling non-profit is the Participatory Culture Foundation, working for a more democratic media landscape
  • Miro, PCF's flagship project, is one of the largest open-source software projects in the world, with 2 million monthly users
  • Projects like Universal Subtitles open up partnership opportunities for PPF’s open data & free engagement tools
 

We have a good start, with big plans

PPF's goal is to increase our non-profit organization's resources and thereby our output of free tools for political participation.

Our vision is of a more "participatory politics" through technology: not only open data for government transparency, but also user-friendly web apps for millions of people worldwide to be meaningfully involved in their communities.

This is the potential of free software on the open Web: a more deliberative democracy, reduced corruption and increased faith in government, better public policy outcomes, and healthier communities. Our needs include the following:

 

“Everyone can be an insider”

  • Funding support for core maintenance of our massive database on the open Web, which fulfills millions of automated requests for information every week.
  • Improve our new features: Contact-Congress, the easiest way to email all three of your members of Congress; and MyOC Groups, for grassroots organizing around bills and issues.
  • Get members of Congress engaged on the site. Turn OC into a two-way platform for continual constituent communication and a forum for participatory budgeting.
 
  • Funding to roll out to remaining U.S. states by Nov. 2012 elections, including wholly-unique features for constituents to email their members of state legislatures.
  • Bring OG down to the city level by incorporating municipal government data, aiming to support dozens of major cities & more localities
  • Expand OG abroad in partnership with #opengov advocates in other countries, as well as transparency for international institutions like the U.N. and E.U.


“Finally, a version of OpenCongress for state legislatures.”

 



User-focused Web tools for the entire civic experience:

  • Open standards for deliberative democracy: collaborative ranking of policy options & participatory budgeting in semantic public forums with widely-accessible design
  • Constituent communication: continual, responsive feedback loops with government officials & legislators about issues & priorities in your community and every neighborhood

New PPF Project Sketches - Help Us Grow and Build These

  1. (PPF + PCF) collaboration: revamped AdTracker for 2012 federal elections, an open platform for tracking campaign videos built on open-source Miro Community software and incorporating information, context, and public opinion from OpenCongress (for federal races) & OpenGovernment (for state & city elections). Proposed video hub will meet public demand for authoritative official information, collaborative fact-checking, campaign donation data, interest group analysis, and improving civility of political discourse. This is yet another much-needed and immediately-useful open-source product for the public benefit.
  2. State, city, and local dashboards of constituent opinion - currently, hyper-local Web portals don’t offer a digestible overview of what political issues constituents care about and specific bills or community issues on which they’re communicating with officials. From users who opt-in to register with their address, OpenCongress can display civic actions at the level of states, cities (5-digit zip codes), down to city blocks (zip plus-four). It’s a powerful new view towards accountability in government.
  3. Money-in-politics message-builder for Rootstrikers & other reformers - new features will make it easier than ever to enforce public accountability “with teeth”. Users can alert elected officials that they’re tracking campaign contributions, down to specific donations from businesses & lobbyists, then organizing other constituents around the bills & issues they support.
  4. Additional project areas include campaigns around the following: range voting, net neutrality, non-partisan re-districting, comprehensive electoral reform, universal voter registration, right-to-vote laws, revoking corporate personhood, and encouraging independent political parties for a more parliamentary system & substantively representative democracy.

Background: with OpenCongress, we took webpages such as the one at right, for 2009's major health-care reform bill on THOMAS.loc.gov :
  • No permalinks to bill pages at the time
  • No RSS feeds of bill actions at the time
  • No social sharing tools at the time
  • No standardized data, no bulk access
  • No user-focused search functionality
  • User-unfriendly, unappealing Web design























… and turned it into webpages like the one shown at left, re-designing the information display, adding useful data sources and free public engagement tools:

  • Welcoming look & feel for user experience
  • Permalinks & commenting on bill text sections
  • RSS feeds & tracking features via “MyOC”
  • Built-in social sharing tools
  • Remixable open-source code & open standards
  • Money-in-politics info on bills, where avail.
  • OC Summaries & issue group analysis of bills
  • Wiki summary spaces & vote visualizations
  • Responsive search functionality
  • Personal voting “aye / nay” on bills
  • Public comment forums - (very popular feature)
  • One-click buttons to contact your elected officials and see what other constituents say

Engagement case study: health care reform debate (April 2010 blog post): from July 2009 through March 2010, over 2,021,575 people visited OC pages for the four bills that came together to form the final law. 1,128,570 of them clicked through to read the official bill text and approx. 169,000 clicked to contact their senators or representative from pages.

Engagement case study: unemployment benefits (March 2008 , Sept. 2009, ongoing blog coverage) - over the past three Congresses, the eight major bills on unemployment insurance gathered close to 175,000 total user comments. As with OpenCongress, the status quo with state legislative websites is a mess of unstandardized, user-unfriendly pages with no accessible context, such as this Louisiana bill page:
























...which we turned into the sample bill page at left on OpenGovernment, with user-focused design and the same one-click engagement tools as featured on OpenCongress. OpenGovernment is the first open-source & non-profit website to bring together official information, campaign contribution data, social wisdom through news & blog coverage, and public comments in one place.

The site’s main categories of information are bills, votes (with full roll call visualizations), legislators (both upper & lower chambers), committees, issue areas, businesses and individual donors from FollowTheMoney & MAPLight.org. Additional context is provided by special interest group analysis, videos (using the open-source Miro Community platform), community wiki project and “key vote” summaries from Project VoteSmart.
